Work-related accidents kill nearly three million people each year and cost an estimated 4\% of global GDP, yet the CCTV networks already installed across industrial sites still depend on human observers whose sustained attention collapses within 20--35 minutes. Automated PPE detection could close that gap, but published systems are trained and evaluated on construction-centric benchmarks and, to our knowledge, none has been evaluated on imagery from a real, operating chemical plant. This paper presents a PPE compliance pipeline developed for and evaluated at OCP Group phosphate-processing facilities. A YOLOv8n detector (3.0M parameters) is fine-tuned at $640\times640$ resolution on a composite industrial dataset --- the public Roboflow PPE-Detection corpus, SH17, a dedicated respirator dataset, and weighted site-specific OCP imagery --- covering the gas masks, CCTV viewing angles, and site uniforms that construction benchmarks omit. The central experiment compares this industrial-adapted model against a detector trained only on the standard construction benchmark: on held-out chemical-plant imagery the adapted model reaches \phX{} mAP@50 against \phX{} for the generic baseline, with consistent gains on an industrial proxy test set --- evidence that generic benchmark training does not transfer to operating-plant conditions, and that the contribution is domain-specific rather than architectural. Every flagged detection is verified by a Qwen2.5-VL judge that states its reason before an alert is dispatched, guarding safety staff against false-alarm fatigue. The pipeline requires no new camera hardware, offering a retrofit path to fatigue-free compliance monitoring.
