\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The central lesson of this paper is that generic benchmark training does not transfer to an operating chemical plant: what a public construction-benchmark model delivers on plant imagery, and what an industrially adapted model delivers on the same frames, are different quantities, and the difference is the contribution. A second, systems-level lesson follows: a detector that flags a missing helmet is not yet a safety system. This section examines both design decisions --- adapting the detector to the environment it must watch, and verifying its outputs before they reach a human --- then states the limits of the present evaluation.

\subsection{Why Domain Adaptation Matters}

Public PPE corpora are dominated by construction imagery: near-frontal views, daylight, helmets and high-visibility vests \cite{roboflowppe,ppeyolo2024}. A phosphate-processing site violates each assumption --- airborne dust lowers contrast, fixed CCTV cameras watch from high oblique angles at distances curated datasets rarely contain, and gas masks and goggles matter as much as helmets. The composite dataset answers these mismatches: fusion broadens pose, scale, and class coverage beyond any single source \cite{ahmad2024sh17}, the respirator source covers the class most specific to chemical processing, and weighted fine-tuning on the self-annotated OCP corpus closes the remaining site gap. The generic-versus-adapted comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results-comparison}) is the experiment this dataset was built for, and it is what elevates the work from another YOLO variant to a domain-specific contribution: the claim is not a better architecture but evidence about what operating-plant imagery demands of training data.

\subsection{Trade-offs of the LLM Judge Stage}

The systems-level half of the argument is that detection alone does not produce a safety system; it relocates the vigilance problem. An unverified detector converts a monitoring burden into an alert-triage burden: safety staff receive a stream in which false positives dominate, habituate to it, and eventually miss the true events, the same decrement documented for CCTV operators \cite{donald2015,sawin1995}. Verification is what makes an alert worth acting on, which is why the judge sits inside the pipeline rather than beside it.

The stage has real costs. Each flagged event incurs a vision-language-model inference measured in seconds; verification runs off the per-frame path, so throughput is unaffected but notification is delayed. More serious is the asymmetry of judge errors: a rejected true violation is costlier than the false alert it would have replaced, so over-suppression is the risk that most needs measurement. Ollama-hosted Qwen2.5-VL \cite{qwen25vl} can run on-premises, keeping frames inside the site network; the pilot used cloud hosting, but on-premises serving is the intended production configuration. The measurement path for the judge is concrete --- a labeled alert set marked by site safety staff, a confirm/reject confusion matrix against that ground truth, false-reject rate as the safety-critical headline --- and is scheduled as future work.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Small and distant objects:} gloves and goggles occupy few pixels in wide CCTV views, and the nano-scale detector loses accuracy on them well before helmets or vests are affected.
  \item \textbf{Dust and weather:} heavy dust, rain, and low light plausibly push accuracy below the reported figures; robustness under these conditions has not been measured.
  \item \textbf{Gas-mask scarcity:} augmentation raises the sample count but not the diversity of the class, which remains under-represented relative to its importance in chemical processing.
  \item \textbf{Comparison confounds:} the generic baseline is trained on far less data than the fused corpus, so the comparison measures what deploying the public benchmark model actually yields on plant imagery, not the isolated effect of domain coverage; a size-matched control is future work.
  \item \textbf{Judge evaluation:} qualitative only. No false-positive-reduction figure has been measured, and none is claimed.
  \item \textbf{Camera placement:} the system inherits the blind spots of the existing CCTV layout; better detection cannot compensate for uncovered areas.
  \item \textbf{OCP imagery release:} the self-annotated site corpus and fine-tuning stage are complete, but public release of OCP frames remains restricted; published qualitative comparisons are limited by imagery-clearance constraints.
\end{itemize}
